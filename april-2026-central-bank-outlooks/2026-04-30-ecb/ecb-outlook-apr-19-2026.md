## Macro Backdrop

As of Apr 17, 2026, the ECB faces stagflationary pressure from the Iran war energy shock (Hormuz blockade Apr 12, Brent >$103). March HICP flash 2.5% (up from 1.9%). GC held at March 19.

Rhetoric shifted hawkish late March through early April (Nagel 'April hike an option,' Villeroy 'next move highly likely upwards,' de Guindos 'hold-or-hike'), but Lagarde's Apr 14 Bloomberg interview marked a notable pullback: 'not enough yet to warrant leaning toward raising rates,' reining in colleagues who ventured hike predictions.

Villeroy's Apr 13 firm-pricing survey added urgency (second-round effects emerging), but Lagarde's walkback outweighs it for the April meeting specifically. Schnabel still 'no rush.'

The Apr 16 publication of the March 18-19 meeting accounts reinforced the consensus: members 'broadly agreed' on the hold, preferring to 'keep rates unchanged...while retaining optionality for future meetings' and accepting near-term above-target inflation (2026 headline projected at 2.6%) provided medium-term expectations remain anchored. No formal dissents were recorded. The accounts documented concerns about second-round effects (workers recouping real-wage losses, firms pre-announcing price rises across detergents/tires/building materials) but the committee's posture was 'meeting-by-meeting approach with no pre-commitment as to the rate path.'

IMF Spring Meetings Apr 14-19: Lagarde and Cipollone in Washington; Lane at UVA macro/finance roundtable (no published text); Schnabel at IIF deglobalization/fragmentation panel; Buch at IIF 'In Conversation' (supervisory context, not rate-path signal).

Net: April hold is baseline, backed by the published accounts; hike tail narrowed materially after Lagarde walkback; June is the more likely first-hike date if data continues deteriorating. Stournaras and Centeno remain the dovish minority.
